# How to Plan Your Life — Workbook

Source: Peterson, *How to Plan Your Life: Vision and Plan, Theory and Practice* (6 lectures). The practical core of Lectures 4–6, with the conceptual frame from 1–3 implicit. Four moves, in his order.

**How to use this file.** Open it in an editor and write your answers in the blank space after each prompt. The point is to do the work, not read about it. Peterson’s stated minimum for the central moves is 15 minutes each; the Future Authoring studies that informed this course used about 90 minutes total of unstructured writing. Going deeper takes longer. Don’t rush; don’t sanitize.

-----

## Phase 1 — Hell-Vision

*Lectures 4–5.*

The aim is to put fear behind you instead of in front of you. To do that, the worst version of you in five years has to be real enough — deeply embodied, not abstract — that you don’t want to go there. Sweat-on-your-back vivid. If you find yourself thinking “I’d never do that,” that’s the move Peterson explicitly tells you to disabuse yourself of. Stay in it.

### 1.1 The stupid things you do that you know are stupid

Not the things you do that you don’t know are stupid (those are blind spots, not the target). The ones you know about. The ones you could stop, but for whatever reason don’t. List them.

### 1.2 The shape of your temptation

Temperament tilts each person toward a different domain. Disagreeable + extroverted tilts toward narcissism and exploitation. High neuroticism tilts toward despair or anxiety-driven avoidance. High openness can tilt toward intellectual evasion, dissociation, addiction to novelty. Agreeableness can tilt toward self-erasure and quiet resentment. What does yours tilt toward? Be specific to you, not the trait description.

### 1.3 Five years of letting it have free rein

Stop resisting the items in 1.1. Let them take you wherever they’re actually aiming. Five years out:

- Where are you living?
- Who are you with — or are you alone?
- How does your face look in the mirror?
- What did you betray to get here?
- What did you lose that you can’t get back?
- What does the person you were five years ago think of you now?

Make it real. Daydream it. Embodied.

### 1.4 The handbasket question, and its inversion

In which handbasket would you ride to which particular hell — and what are you doing right now that increases that probability?

Then Peterson’s inversion from Lecture 4. Whenever you’re afraid of something and you’re inclined to avoid, you can ask yourself the opposite question: *what are the consequences of not doing it?* You think you’re afraid of confronting your boss, your partner, your body, your debt, the conversation, the truth. Fair enough. How afraid are you of *not* confronting it? Of laboring under it for another five years? Ten? Thirty?

Peterson’s bus analogy: you wait half an hour for a bus that should have come in five minutes. You don’t want to leave because you’ve already wasted half an hour. So you wait another two hours. People do this with decades.

What are you not doing, and what’s the bus you’ve been waiting at?

## Phase 2 — Presumption of Innocence

*Lecture 5. Brief, but the next phase doesn’t work without it.*

Phase 3 won’t work if you start it punishing yourself. The presumption of innocence is the standing legal premise — innocent until proven guilty, by yourself. You don’t have to feel worthy. You just have to grant the working assumption that it would be OK if things turned out well for you.

### 2.1 What buoyed you when things were worst

Look back. The hardest stretches of your life — what did you turn to that kept you from going under? What gave you solace or strength? A relationship, a duty, an interest, a faith, a beauty, a piece of work? Be specific. These are the fingerprints of what you actually find meaningful, not what you say you find meaningful.

### 2.2 The case for your own worth

If you had to make it out loud, in front of someone hostile, how would you make it? Flawed as you are, what about you is worth advocating for?

### 2.3 Through someone else’s lens

Peterson’s specific exercise from Lecture 5. Most people can’t extend the benefit of the doubt to themselves directly. They can do it for someone else. So borrow that capacity.

Pick someone you actually care for — a sister, brother, child, parent, friend. Someone you would advocate for if they were attacked. Hold that person in mind. Now see if you can see yourself through that lens.

Peterson’s point: at least half the population values other people more than they value themselves. They’ll give other people a break and the benefit of the doubt when they won’t do that to themselves at all. If you’re one of them, you already have the advocate-capacity in you — you’ve just been refusing to point it at one particular person. The fix isn’t narcissism. It’s ascribing to yourself the same worth you would ascribe to someone you care for. If they have value, then you have value.

Who did you pick? And if they were treating themselves the way you’ve been treating yourself, what would you say to them?

### 2.4 Grant the assumption

Take whatever time you need — fifteen minutes, an hour, a day. Assume it would be OK if things worked out for you. Hold that frame as you enter Phase 3.

## Phase 3 — Heaven-Vision

*Lectures 5–6. The most substantial phase.*

Five moves. Start by noticing what’s already calling to you. Then the open sweep. Then the differentiated one across the dimensions of life Peterson walks through in Lecture 6. Then a check from the back. Then climb down from five years to this week.

### 3.1 The open sweep

Five years from now, if you got what you wanted and needed — if you were treating yourself properly — what would your life look like? Don’t sanitize. Including intensity, ambition, things you’d be ashamed to admit. What would actually satisfy a creature like you?

And underneath that: what would justify the struggle and the death you’re going to have? You need something big enough to bear that weight.

### 3.2 What already calls to you

*Lecture 6. Before you decide what you want, notice what’s already calling. The burning bush teaching.*

Peterson’s claim from Lecture 6: interest is not something you create. It comes upon you. It makes itself manifest. You can squelch it or fail to follow it, but you can’t elicit it from nothing. The grip of interest is, in Peterson’s words, “a manifestation of the instinct that drives you to learn” — the signal that you’re on the edge of what you know, where transformation happens.

Peterson uses the burning bush as the image. Moses is wandering, doing his job. Something catches his attention off to the side. *It’s not an oak tree on fire in the middle of his path. It’s something he could pass.* But he deviates and investigates. The whole story turns on that deviation.

The question is what’s been burning at the side of your road that you keep walking past.

- What do you read about, watch, drift toward, that you wouldn’t put on a CV?
- Where does your attention go when you should be doing something else?
- What has called you persistently across years — through three jobs, two relationships, four cities — that you’ve never given a real chance?
- What did you love as a child or teenager that you’ve talked yourself out of since?
- What burns at the side of the road that you keep passing?

Don’t filter for plausibility, income, or what your parents would think. The differentiated sweep below will do that filtering. Right now you’re collecting evidence about what already has autonomy over your attention. Peterson’s warning: if you lie to yourself, you’ll pervert this signal, and you’ll follow false idols instead.

### 3.3 The differentiated sweep

Eight dimensions. Peterson’s list. For each: not what you want abstractly, but what it specifically looks like, and who you would have to be to credibly have it. Carry forward what surfaced in 3.2 — if something called to you there, it should make itself felt in one or more of these dimensions.

#### 3.3.1 Intimate relationship

What does the relationship look like, day-to-day? Not the wedding photo — the Tuesday morning. Who would you have to be to make the offer that would attract the partner you’d actually want? What do you have to offer? What would satisfy you sexually, emotionally, intellectually — including what’s shadow-shaped? (Peterson is explicit on this: if you sanitize, you’ll be unsatisfied and looking outside the relationship.)

#### 3.3.2 Family

Children — yes, no, how many, when, what relationship with them, what kind of parent are you? If no children: what fills the second half of life? Parents and siblings — what relationship do you want with them, and what work would that require from you?

#### 3.3.3 Friends

What sort of friends? Peterson’s rules of thumb: people you can tell good news to without them substituting their own. People you can tell bad news to without them topping it with their misery. People aiming up alongside you. People you can have adventures with. Who fits? Who needs to be let go? What are you offering as a friend?

#### 3.3.4 Work

What justifies the sacrifices you’ll make for this work? Not “what do I want to do” in the abstract, but what kind of contribution and what kind of life does the work make possible? If your current work doesn’t fit the vision, what does? If it does fit, what does the stellar version of your current role look like?

#### 3.3.5 Learning outside work

Three to four things you could learn over the next five years. Specific. Things that would change what you can do, what you’re interesting about, who you can be in a room. Cross-check against 3.2 — does what’s calling to you have a learning track inside it?

#### 3.3.6 Physical and psychological self-care

What does taking care of yourself look like? Not just gym. Sleep, diet, what goes into your head, contemplative practice, body work, medical work. What would be enough?

#### 3.3.7 Temptation strategy

Your specific one — from 1.2. What’s your strategy (not a vow, a strategy) for keeping it inside lines that don’t wreck the rest? Peterson’s diagnostic: if you can’t control a temptation, what you’re aiming at is probably not good enough to crowd it out. So: is what you’ve written above big enough?

#### 3.3.8 Civic and community responsibility

What service do you owe the broader community? Political, religious, neighborhood, professional, charitable. If you don’t take it on, someone who wants power will. What’s your share?

### 3.4 The check from the back

Read 3.1 through 3.3 together. Is what you’ve written here actually bigger and more demanding than what you imagined in Phase 1?

Peterson’s point: false adventure (drinking, drugs, affairs, licentious troublemaking) substitutes when the real adventure isn’t big enough. If the heaven-vision is smaller than the hell-vision, the heaven-vision is too cautious.

What needs to be larger?

If the answer is anything other than “nothing — heaven is bigger,” go back to 3.1 and 3.3 and rewrite the parts that came out timid. Then return here. This is not optional. A heaven-vision that loses to your hell-vision is not enough motivation to change a Tuesday, let alone a life.

### 3.5 The ladder down

*Five years is too far to act on. Climb it down. The point is to find, at each rung, what would have to be true for the 5-year vision in 3.1–3.4 to still be on the table.*

Work backward, not forward. “Where will I be” is a guess. “What would have to be true” is a derivation.

- **In 3 years.** Of the eight dimensions, which two or three carry the heaviest load between now and there? What does each look like?
- **In 1 year.** What’s visible by then? Who has joined your life? Who has left? What’s begun? What’s ended?
- **In 3 months.** What’s the closest milestone you could point to and say “that’s evidence I’m on the path”? Specific enough to know whether you hit it or missed it.
- **This month.** What gets done. Not “worked on.” Done.
- **This week.** What’s the one thing whose absence by Sunday would mean you’re already off the path?

## Phase 4 — Schedule as Invitation

*Lecture 6, with the day-design teaching from Lecture 2.*

The schedule is where the vision lands. It’s not tyranny imposed on you. It’s the day you would actually want, derived from the vision you just wrote. Done right, you wake up to it as opportunity.

### 4.1 The twenty that repeat

*Lecture 6. Peterson’s arithmetic: a 20-minute transition you do daily is roughly 5% of your life — about 12 working days a year. Get 20 things like that right, he says, and your whole life is right. Most people obsess over the exceptional (vacations, special occasions) while the repeating moments quietly set the tone for everything else.*

List your repeating moments. Be specific — not “morning routine” but the actual sequence of things you actually do. Examples to think against, not copy:

- The first 10 minutes after you open your eyes
- The first cup of coffee, or what stands in for it
- The work-start — how you cross from not-working to working
- The first work block
- Lunch — who with, where, what
- The 20-minute transition from work back to the rest of your life (Peterson’s specific example)
- Re-entry into the house — what’s the first thing you say, and to whom
- Dinner
- Bedtime with children, if applicable
- The hour before you sleep
- The first 10 minutes after you and your partner are both home, awake, and unoccupied
- The Sunday evening
- The weekly food shop, the weekly clean, the weekly call to your parents
- The walk, the workout, the practice — whatever happens three or more times a week

### 4.2 Design the day you’d want

Plan tomorrow specifically. Not a generic weekday — tomorrow. The repeating moment you identified in 4.1 should be present and treated, not endured. The thing on your “this week” line from 3.5 should have a time slot.

- What time do you get up?
- How do you get up — mood, first thing you do?
- What’s breakfast?
- What’s the first work block, on what?
- When does the 4.1 moment you’re repairing happen, and what’s different about it tomorrow?
- When does Phase 3’s vision actually get time in this day? Which dimensions are present in tomorrow, in concrete form?
- When do you eat, with whom, where?
- What’s the evening — work, learning, family, rest, in what proportion?
- When do you sleep?

### 4.3 Nietzsche’s move

Before anything else: what unpleasant thing is morally required of you to do tomorrow, to keep the ledger straight? Name it specifically. Do it first.

### 4.4 The two anchor habits

Concrete and small. Skip if you already do them.

- **Bed, first thing.** One small disorder confronted; one small order produced. Peterson means it literally.
- **Ten minutes putting one thing in order, somewhere in your space.** Daily. Cumulatively, the whole space in a year.

Note where these would land in 4.2.

### 4.5 The week-trial commitment

Peterson’s term is collaborative empiricism. Run the schedule for a week as a hypothesis, not a vow. See what worked and what didn’t. Adjust the schedule, not yourself. Then run another week.

## Closing

Peterson’s actual position from Lecture 6, condensed: you’re going to act out either the proposition that confronting chaos honestly produces order that’s good, or its contrary. There is no third option. The vision and schedule above are the act-out.

Reviewing this document is not the doing of the work. The writing into the spaces is.
